Part 1: Learn what type of career you want

Goal: Go from "I want to work with food" to "I think I want to work in formulation for alt-protein."

It's natural to want to be in the food business because you love food, eating, and/or cooking. Check in with yourself that you don't confuse the enjoyment of food with enjoying a food business career.

For example, you might enjoy cooking at home or throwing dinner parties occasionally and not want to run a restaurant as a job. How do you find that out? By gathering data through:

Independent Research

I recommend spending 30 minutes a day for at least 2 weeks seriously researching the industry.

  • Industry-insider newsletters:
    • AgFunderNews
    • FoodDive
    • FoodTechWeekly
  • Podcasts: My Food Job Rocks - Listen to Adam Yee's interviews with product developers, research chefs, QC/QA experts, food safety specialists, sensory scientists, and supply chain specialists.
  • Books:
    • Hooked by Michael Moss
    • Flavorama by Arielle Johnson

Informational Interviews

Write to people and invite them for virtual or in-person coffee chats/walks. Don't try to impress the other person. You don't even know if they're worth impressing yet! The goal is to learn their story and get inspired by their authentic journey.

Attending Events

Attend industry conferences, meetups, and networking events. This gives you a sense of what the environment is like and who the key players are in the space.

Part 2: Get a foot in the door

Goal: Land your first role in food science and build real experience.

Bridge Resume

Craft a resume that bridges your current background to food science. Highlight transferable skills like analytical abilities, project management, attention to detail, and any relevant coursework or self-study. If you don't have direct food science experience, emphasize adjacent skills and your genuine interest in the field.

Short Term Experience

Look for internships, contract roles, or entry-level positions like QA/QC technician, food safety coordinator, or production associate. These don't always require deep experience and can be great starting points. Even a few months of hands-on experience will give you credibility and help you understand what you actually enjoy doing.

Part 3: Iterate and expand into a full career

Goal: Build on your initial experience and move toward your ideal role.

After your first role, you'll have a much clearer picture of what excites you and what doesn't. Use this clarity to make strategic next moves. Seek roles that play to your strengths and align with the career you envision. Keep learning, stay connected to your network, and be open to unexpected opportunities.
