Learn what type of career you want

Goal: Go from "I want to work with food" to "I think I want to work in formulation for alt-protein."

It’s natural to want to be in the food business because you love food, eating, and/or cooking. But don’t confuse the enjoyment of food with enjoying an operational career in food. The former is arguably necessary, but definitely not sufficient, for the latter. For example, you might enjoy cooking at home or throwing dinner parties occasionally and not want to run a restaurant as a job.

How do you find out whether a job in food science is for you? By answering the following 2 questions:

1. What do you actually want to do on a daily basis?

2. What do I want my output to be?

Caveat: Question #2 isn’t what do I want my organization’s output to be? Your org might produce something cool, but your job is to write reports that no one reads. You’ll burn out if you stay for the name or coolness factor alone. A better situation would be to be in an org that makes something that you don’t care much about, but your day-to-day job is actually really interesting. It would be great, at some point, to be enthusiastic about both the day-to-day tasks and the broad direction of the company.


To answer the two questions above, run low-stakes micro-experiments to gather data.

Why low-stakes? Because most people find moving into a new industry intimidating and won’t try it if there is a lot to lose.

Why micro? Because you don’t actually know if the path you’re on is worth pursuing or worth pivoting from. Take small steps, get data, and then decide if you want to make bigger commitments.

Independent Research

I recommend spending 30 minutes a day for at least 2 weeks seriously researching the industry.

  • Industry-insider newsletters:
    • AgFunderNews
    • FoodDive
    • FoodTechWeekly
  • Podcasts: My Food Job Rocks - Listen to Adam Yee's interviews with all sorts of food people. Ask yourself, do I want to do what they're doing?
  • Books:
    • Hooked by Michael Moss
      • - Important to understand how food science knowledge can be weaponized.
    • Flavorama by Arielle Johnson
    • Taste What You're Missing by Barb Stuckey

Informational Chats

Write to people and invite them for virtual or in-person coffee chats/walks. Don’t try to impress the other person. You don’t even know if they’re worth impressing yet! The goal is to get information that informs whether you want to keep pursuing the current path or to course correct. Yes, of course you’re being subtly judged. If there is organically a fit, the person will naturally point you to a hiring manager or an HR person. But I find that it usually doesn’t help to focus on that as the outcome.

  • Who to contact, in order of priority:
    • People who’re doing the exact job that you want to do
    • People who are in the industry, maybe not in the exact niche that you want, but at least they’re likely to talk to you. This could be your friend’s sister’s climbing partner who you ran into at the gym once. Reach out to them.
  • How to reach out: I write as I’d like to be written to. I say who I am, why I want to talk to the person, and then ask if they want to talk. Make it easy for the person being asked to say yes. Here are some examples from my inbox:
  • What to say, once you're on the call:
    • Thank you so much for taking the time to chat with me. I really appreciate your time. I am currently a [X], and I’m exploring working in the food industry. Can I ask you a bunch of questions?
    • Questions that help you understand their day-to-day tasks:
      • How did you end up doing this work?
      • What’s a typical day like for you?
      • What do you like the most about your job?
      • What do you like the least? How do you deal with that?
      • Where do you think your next job will be?
    • Questions that trigger the other person to problem solve on your behalf:
      • I’ve found that I really enjoy X. How much of X exists in your job?
      • I have a background in Y and skills in A, B, and C. Given your experience, what orgs or positions do you think would be a good fit for me?
      • I have a background in Y and skills in A, B, and C. If I wanted to work in your position, what skills or experience do I need that I currently don’t have? How can I get those skills?
      • Is there anyone else in your network that you think would be helpful for me to talk to? If they suggest someone, then ask: would you be comfortable making an introduction?
      • Are there other orgs that you think I should take a look at, given my background and interests? If they suggest something, do you know anyone there that might be willing to chat with me?


Note: The last section of questions might feel harder for you to ask. They’re certainly harder for the person to answer, because they can’t just say how they feel. But their answers are the most valuable to you, because you’re asking them to rally their years of experience to figure out a viable path that could work for you. You want to get them into a position where they’re like, you know what, this might not work, but here’s what I’d try if I were you... Not everybody is capable of doing this, as it requires them to want to help and put themselves into your shoes and be a problem solver on your behalf. Don’t get discouraged if some people give you dud answers.

Attending Events

Great for meeting a lot of people all at once. You get a quick overview of entire ecosystems of companies and sectors and the dizzying variety of products that food scientists touch.

  • Institute of Food Technologists:If you’re able to, go to the big annual one that usually takes place in Chicago. Or check out the gatherings organized by your local chapter, which are often more fruitful because they’re more intimate, less overwhelming, and you get a sense of the specific companies/sectors to your geographic region. For example, flavor houses are concentrated in the NJ area. Chicago is a hub for consumer packaged goods. In Washington State, there are a lot of beer and wine people.
  • Research Chefs Association

2. Get a foot in the door

Goal: Land your first role in food science and build real experience.

Bridge Resume

TK

  • Highlight food experience by renaming “Work Experience” as “Relevant Experience:” When I dig into clients’ backgrounds, we almost always realize that they have experience working with food, even though it wasn’t always paid nor a “proper” job. By renaming this section “Relevant Experience,” the filter opens up for what makes it in. You can include all sorts of extra-curriculars in which you’ve already been pursuing your interest in food. You get to showcase a proven history of experience and interest in this area.
  • Highlight transferable skills by finding the shared DNA between your previous work and the food industry: When you stay in your own industry, you zoom in because micro-specificity makes you stand out against generalists. But specificity becomes counterproductive when you cross borders. The food hiring manager doesn’t care that you know how FDA regulation Section blahblah applies to the presence of lead in paint. They do care that you know how to comply with federal regulatory standards. When you cross industry borders, you have to zoom out until you find the exact altitude where your skills and the new industry’s needs overlap.

    For example: Say you studied chemistry, currently work a junior QC job at a pharma company, and want to get into food. You can write a bullet point 3 ways:

    Too specific: "Analyzed active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) in pediatric acetaminophen batches using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) to ensure strict compliance with FDA Title 21 CFR Part 211."

    Too broad: "Responsible for checking product quality and making sure the company followed all government regulations."

    Sweet spot: "Executed daily quality control protocols for high-volume batch manufacturing, utilizing HPLC to verify chemical consistency and ensure compliance with FDA standards."

    After reading the “sweet spot” version, the food hiring manager will think, “oh good, this person already knows how to follow SOPs, run an HPLC machine, and handle data in a high-stakes manufacturing setting. If they can do it for medicine, that gives me confidence that they can do it for our beverage line.”

    I've tried to make this as simple as possible, because I don't want the article to be a thousand pages. Creating a good bridge resume actually takes some thought, and we're getting into an area where it can be helpful to have support. When I work with clients on this, we usually spend a couple weeks progressing through 4-5 drafts to make sure that this is really going to be effective.     Pivot Pitch

    When you pivot into a new industry, you don't have a past track record in that field to point to. You must use your past to build a believable trajectory. This is a coherent, high-agency 90-sec story that explains three things:

    1. Where you came from (background and skills)
    2. Why you left (intentional decision point)
    3. Why this specific destination is the logical next step (alignment)

    For example: Let's continue with the junior QC analyst scenario from the section above.

    The Accidental Tourist: "Well, I studied chemistry in college. I didn't know what to do other than work in pharma after graduation. Then I got bored. But I’ve always liked baking at home and watching food shows, so maybe I’d enjoy working in food science more..."

    The Thoughtful Navigator: "I spent the last two years in pharma QC mastering high-volume batch protocols and FDA compliance. I mastered the day-to-day operations and realized I wanted to apply that technical baseline to a physical substrate I'm more interested in: food products. Given my background in chemical production, moving into food product QC seems to be a logical intersection of my skills and my interests."

    The Accidental Tourist version comes across as unfocused and passive. You're pinballing here and there with no internal direction. It's hard to help a person who doesn't know what they want. The Thoughtful Navigator version frames your pivot as an deliberate evolution. When you present a plan that draws a line from your past straight into the lap of the person sitting across from you, that person buys into the plan and gets excited to help. By the way, it's ok to explore multiple options at once. You can say, "I've narrowed down a few different paths and am gathering more data to decide which one to further pursue." Name that you're being intentionally experimental, not clueless.

    Land a short term work engagement (aka a small, non-glorious job)

    Unleash your Bridge Resume and 90-Second Pivot Pitch to land a small, non-glorious job.

    Think of the food industry as a country like the US. If your dream is to end up in Seattle, Washington because you want to be close to the water, it’s awfully specific to try to get there in one go. Instead, you just need to cross the border into an adjacent state. Get a low-commitment, foot-in-the-door role that gets you inside the border.

    Once inside, two things will happen:

    Your visibility explodes. You get a much better sense of the landscape, the jobs that exist, and where the opportunities are.

    You gain legitimacy. You are no longer an outsider trying to get in. You are an insider looking to make a successive jump.

    Look for small businesses.

    Avoid applying for structured, highly competitive internship programs at Nestle or Kraft. These corporate giants have rigid recruiting pipelines tied directly to university food science departments. Instead, look for small businesses, boutique firms, and local startups.

    Why small outfits are a good bet:

    • Fewer gatekeepers: Small firms, if they're thriving, generally have more work than people. If you show up competent and proactive, they'll take your help. There are no automated HR software or complex multi-tiered interview process. You can often meet the founder or lab director at a meet-up, grab their email, and directly pitch them on an internship.
    • You learn more: In a massive corporation, you might be trapped all summer testing the sodium levels of one cracker. In a 5-person office, you might be hired for one thing, but you are sitting three feet away from the founder who’s talking high level strategy and the R&D director who just came back from a plant visit. You get a fly-on-the-wall view of the entire business.


    The magical-thinking-elf route:

    This is an extreme version of the strategy that I describe above. I’m referring to finding the 1 or 2 people/teams in the world that are doing exactly what you want and going after them. It likely won’t work. But if you apply this strategy often enough, you’re bound to hit a home run once. For example, early on in my career, there were a few teams that I really admired: Zero Point Zero, the company that produced Anthony Bourdain’s shows; Roads & Kingdoms, the publisher of Rice, Noodle, Fish, a book with a writing style that I wanted to apply to Chinese cuisine; Modernist Cuisine, which blended science and the culinary arts in a mind-blowing way; later on, Patagonia Provisions, where I saw deep potential to apply daring, unproven food science innovations in service of sustainability. I stalked the people on these teams. I pitched myself to them. I found email addresses and wrote cringe-worthily earnest treatises on why they should let me work for them. The team at Modernist Cuisine replied, which, after an initial no, then a maybe, then a no, then finally an interview, eventually turned into a job offer. As for the others, either I eventually lost interest or, in the case of Patagonia Provisions, I got to interact with them enough to ultimately understand that they weren't going were I wanted to go. That allowed me to drop that dream without regrets.

    Don't wait around collecting certifications and degrees and resume points with the idea that someday you'll be worthy.

    If there’s a team you admire, stalk them and offer your services. They might just say yes, and you’ll have created a shortcut to doing the work you’ve always wanted to do.

    Part 3: Iterate and expand into a full career

    Once your short-term work assignment or stage wraps up, you have crossed the border. You are no longer an outsider looking in; you have official industry experience on your resume and real data in your head.

    Analyze the Data

    Now, it’s time to take stock. You look at your time inside the industry and analyze it like a scientist:

    • What tasks gave you energy? (e.g., these are tasks that you can do for a long time and still want to do more of)
    • What tasks drained you? (e.g., these are tasks that you'd prefer not to do, not even once)
    • What hidden sectors did you discover? (e.g., these are the adjacent niches you didn't know existed six months ago)

    Iterate

    Take your learnings from the short term work experience and feed it back into Part 2 of the protocol:

    • Update the Resume: Your "Bridge Resume" is now an "Insider Resume." On top of your new experience, you now get to add a new chunk of concrete food-industry deliverables.
    • Refine the Pitch Pivot: You rewrite your trajectory. It’s no longer about why you want to get into food; it’s about what specific problem you solved inside the food industry this time and where you are taking that expertise next.
    • Make the Successive Jump: You map your next destination. You might realize you want to try a completely different flavor of the industry to see what else fits.

    This is exactly how I built my own non-linear career path, and it’s how my most successful clients do it too. Treat your career not as a single, high-stakes choice you have to get right on the first try, but as a series of successive, intentional jumps. Let yourself discover how fun this can be.
