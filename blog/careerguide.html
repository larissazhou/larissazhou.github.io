How to transition into a food science career
2026-06-19

1. Learn what type of career you want

Goal: Go from "I want to work with food" to "I think I want to work in formulation for alt-protein."

It's natural to want to be in the food business because you love food, eating, and/or cooking. Check in with yourself that you don't confuse the enjoyment of food with enjoying a food business career.

For example, you might enjoy cooking at home or throwing dinner parties occasionally and not want to run a restaurant as a job. How do you find that out? By gathering data through:

Independent Research

I recommend spending 30 minutes a day for at least 2 weeks seriously researching the industry.

  • Industry-insider newsletters:
    • AgFunderNews
    • FoodDive
    • FoodTechWeekly
  • Podcasts: My Food Job Rocks - Listen to Adam Yee's interviews with product developers, research chefs, QC/QA experts, food safety specialists, sensory scientists, and supply chain specialists.
  • Books:
    • Hooked by Michael Moss
    • Flavorama by Arielle Johnson

Informational Interviews

Reach out to 5-10 people working in food science roles. Ask them about their day-to-day work, career path, and what they wish they'd known starting out. Most people are happy to chat for 20-30 minutes if you're genuine and respectful of their time.

Job Shadowing or Plant Tours

Many food companies offer tours or have informational days. This gives you a real sense of what the environment is like and what people actually do.

2. Build Relevant Skills

Goal: Develop expertise that makes you competitive for food science positions.

Depending on the role you're targeting, you may need:

  • Technical knowledge (food chemistry, microbiology, nutrition)
  • Analytical skills (statistical analysis, lab techniques)
  • Product development experience
  • Regulatory knowledge (FDA, FSMA, labeling)
  • Software proficiency (R, Python, Excel, JMP)

Formal Education vs. Self-Study

Consider whether a degree, certification, or self-directed learning makes sense for your timeline and budget. Many roles require a science degree, but you can supplement with specialized courses.

3. Build Your Network

Goal: Create relationships with people in the industry who can provide guidance and opportunities.

Attend industry conferences, join professional organizations (like the Institute of Food Technologists), and engage on LinkedIn. The food industry is surprisingly small and connected.

4. Gain Practical Experience

Goal: Get your foot in the door with internships, entry-level roles, or side projects.

Even if your first role isn't perfect, it gets you experience, references, and a clearer picture of where you want to go next.

Internships

If you're a student, prioritize getting one or more food science internships before graduation.

Entry-Level Roles

QA/QC technician, food safety coordinator, or production associate roles often don't require deep experience and can be great starting points.

Side Projects

Launch a small food product, document the process, and share what you learned. This shows initiative and practical knowledge.

5. Iterate and Refine

Goal: Continuously update your understanding of what you want and what you're capable of.

After each experience, reflect:

  • Did I enjoy this type of work?
  • What energized me? What drained me?
  • What gaps do I need to fill?
  • Is this the right next step?
